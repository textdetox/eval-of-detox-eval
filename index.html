Evaluating Text Style Transfer:
A Nine-language Benchmark for Text Detoxification

1AIRI  ·  2Universidade de Santiago de Compostela  ·  3Technical University of Munich (TUM)  ·  4Munich Center for Machine Learning (MCML)  ·  5Skoltech

Abstract

Reliable multilingual evaluation for text detoxification

Despite notable advances in large language models (LLMs), reliable evaluation of text generation tasks such as text style transfer (TST) remains an open challenge. Existing research has shown that automatic metrics often correlate poorly with human judgments, limiting our ability to assess model performance accurately. Furthermore, most prior work has focused primarily on English, while the evaluation of multilingual TST systems—particularly for text detoxification—remains largely underexplored.

In this paper, we present the first comprehensive multilingual benchmarking study of evaluation metrics for text detoxification across nine languages. Drawing inspiration from machine translation evaluation, we compare neural-based automatic metrics with LLM-as-a-judge approaches together with experiments on task-specific fine-tuned models. Our analysis reveals that the proposed metrics achieve significantly higher correlation with human judgments compared to baseline approaches.

Methodology

A new recipe for detoxification evaluation

Classic detoxification evaluation relied on ChrF for fluency, cosine similarity of LaBSE embeddings for content, and a single toxicity classifier probability for style accuracy — all combined into a joint score J. We identify the core weaknesses and propose targeted improvements for each dimension.

Baseline (J-OLD)
  • ChrF n-gram overlap vs. reference only
  • Input-only cosine similarity (LaBSE)
  • Single classifier probability for toxicity
  • No reference or source context for toxicity
Proposed (J-NEW / J-XCOMET-CLS)
  • XCOMET-lite: semantic triplet (src, hyp, ref)
  • SIM-JOINED: weighted input + reference similarity
  • CLS-NEW: relative toxicity across input, output, ref
  • Penalisation and rewarding stabilisation rules
🧬
Fluency (FL)
XCOMET-lite
New

Replaces ChrF with a compressed COMET model using input–output–reference triplets to assess fluency semantically.

🔗
Content (SIM)
SIM-JOINED
New

Weighted combination of input–output and output–reference cosine similarity (w=0.4 / 0.6), balancing preservation and reference alignment.

⚖️
Style (STA)
CLS-NEW
New

Triplet-based toxicity scoring with penalisation (output more toxic than input → 0) and rewarding (output ≥ reference quality → 1).

Joint metric formula

// Original joint metric J = (1/n) · Σ STA(yᵢ) · SIM(xᵢ, yᵢ) · chrF(xᵢ, yᵢ) // Proposed: STA → CLS-NEW, SIM → SIM-JOINED, FL → XCOMET-lite J-NEW = (1/n) · Σ CLS-NEW(tᵢ, tg, tᵣ) · SIM-JOINED(vᵢ, vg, vᵣ) · XCOMET-lite(src, hyp, ref) // Simplified (XCOMET handles both fluency & content) J-XCOMET-CLS = (1/n) · Σ CLS-NEW(tᵢ, tg, tᵣ) · XCOMET-lite(src, hyp, ref)
Results

Key findings

  • 1
    ChrF fails for detoxification — near-zero Spearman correlations in Arabic, German, Chinese, English, and Ukrainian. Its character n-gram overlap penalises the substantial rewording detoxification inherently requires.
  • 2
    XCOMET-lite matches XCOMET-XXL on fluency across most languages at only 40% of the computational cost — the recommended default for competitions and production systems.
  • 3
    LLMs outperform automatic metrics for fluency in Arabic, Hindi, Russian, and Ukrainian (LLaMA 3.3-70B-Instruct-Turbo leads). Automatic metrics remain stronger for content similarity in most languages.
  • 4
    Fine-tuned Llama-3.1-8B dominates toxicity evaluation — highest correlations in Amharic, Chinese, English, German, and Ukrainian. Fluency fine-tuning benefits are English-centric due to training data imbalance.
  • 5
    J-NEW beats J-OLD in all nine languages, with J-XCOMET-CLS (the simplified version) performing best in Chinese, English, Spanish, and Ukrainian — suggesting XCOMET alone captures both fluency and content.
  • 6
    GPT-4.1-mini is the strongest LLM judge for toxicity across most languages, while DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B achieves the best Russian toxicity correlations by a significant margin.
